Four approaches to resolving conflict

Have you ever been in a situation where you need to reach a decision jointly with another person and you seem stuck? Discussions starts to go round in circles, perhaps become a bit heated. You become frustrated. You notice behaviour that irritates you. You may feel you’re not being listened to…

How best to resolve this? There are a few possible approaches.

Discuss the content – the differences in views

This is usually the natural starting point but it pays to make sure each point-of-view or idea is fully understood and that different views are analysed and compared against what needs to be achieved. This approach is about bringing structure to the jumble of thoughts and create clarity. (Diagramming can help with this – The power of diagramming: clarity and better decisions). This approach may be enough to unblock the conversation and to agree on a way forward.

Shift the focus to interests – the higher purpose at stake

William Ury discusses this approach in his book “Getting to Yes”. It means taking a step back from the details and both of you talking about what is important in the given context, what your motivation, your higher purpose for the situation at hand is. For example, “why do you work for the organisation? What is important to you in this work? Focusing on interests helps to find common ground, and can often be the key to unblocking the situation.

Have the "difficult" conversation – the behaviour and your feelings

This is about addressing the behaviours that bother you and how they impact you. For example, if the other person talks over you. The longer the conflict situation has been going on, the more likely that negative feelings will have emerged. It may seem a big deal to have a “difficult” conversation but it needn’t be. You can find an effective and concise structure for opening the conversation at the end of this article.

Make use of structures

If you can’t come to an acceptable resolution using any of the three previous approaches, you can bring in objective elements such as governance arrangements, responsibilities within a role, duties and accountabilities, agreed processes or generally accepted best practice.

  • If you have some authority based on any of these structures, and thus you are using them as a rationale for your point-of-view, As a way to influence, this approach is less powerful as it may lead the other person to comply rather than really buy into the agreement.
  • However, invoking these structures can help to break through an impasse – since they are objective you can see them as an inanimate arbitrator, and they may make it easier for both of you to let go of initial viewpoints without losing face.

Choose carefully

Options 1 and 2 can be seen as on the “softer” side: working through differences in views and finding common interests. Options 3 and 4 are slightly more assertive as they are about addressing behaviours (and potentially setting boundaries) and invoking authority derived from structures.

So when you’re in a conflict situation, it’s useful to consider these four options and chose carefully. They can be used progressively or combined.

When I recently presented these four approaches during a webinar, there was an audible sigh of relief. One attendee said: “Thank goodness for options, because having a difficult conversation feels daunting to me”.

Bonus: Opening a “difficult” conversation

The key is to keep the introduction brief and to the point. Don’t be apologetic; simply share your experience of the behaviour.

  1. “Focus on the behaviour – be specific: Very briefly, share what exactly the person is doing that causes an issue for you.

  2. “Name the impact on you – your feelings: Simply state how you feel and the impact it has on you. For example, “when you talk over me I feel inferior”.

  3. “Ask them for their perspective: Ask the other person what their perspective on the situation is.

This should give you a good opener – and getting the conversation off on the right track is usually the hardest part.
